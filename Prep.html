Rabbit Prep & Introducing Animals

Bringing a new rabbit home is a big step and transition! We want to make sure you feel prepared for bringing your new family member into their forever home. Addiontally, introducing them to kids, other rabbits, and other animals can be challenging, so here is a resource of where to get started.


Preparation for a Rabbit

First, look around your home and find a place that would be best to let your new furry friend stay. They need their own dedicated space that they can get adjusted to and feel comfortable in.
Second, "bunny-proof" your home. Whether your rabbit free roams or has a play pen to of their own. One thing is for sure, they will try to eat everything, so protect your walls, baseboards, wires, and more. You don't want your stuff destroyed and your rabbit consuming household objects could cause some health concerns.
Third, start getting supplies for your rabbit. You do not need to get everything your rabbit will ever need. Just start with the basics and get what you need as you get to know your bunny!
A good list to start is: litter box, litter, water bowl, food bowl, a few toys, some treats (reference the "Housing & Food" for suggestions), a pen if you choose to not free-roam them, hay, hay pellets, and some fruit and veggies. Additionally, a soft surface, and a hard cool surface, can be a nice addition for your rabbit to warm and cool themselves.

First Night with a Rabbit

Your rabbit will most likely be stressed from the day. Any animal needs there space to adjust. You are a new person in their life and they will need some time to get to know you and trust you. Bring your new rabbit to their space you prepared and give them some time. Let them adjust to their new environement. We reccomend not getting to much in their space at the very beginning, give them time, let them adjust, and let them come to you.
In the beginning, try to minimize sudden movements and really loud noises, however, do the things you normally do in your own home. Your rabbit needs to get used to your life style and the noises that come along with it.
When interacting with your rabbit on that first nights, and subsequent early days, try to not grab them from above. This can startle then and they could perceive you as a hawk.
In summary, co-exist with your rabbit, spend time in the room they are in so they can get used to you, and let them come to you. You don't need to crowd their space.

When to Spay/Neuter Your Rabbit

It is best to spay/neuter your rabbit between 5-6 months of age. For female rabbits, it greatly reduces risks of ovarian and uterine cancers. Additionally, it can help lessen some inconveinent behaviors during this time, such as spraying pee and humping.

How to Introduce a Second Rabbit Or Other Animal

The process of getting rabbits to live with each other peacefully is called bonding. Introducing a second rabbit takes time and patience. Before you introduce a second rabbit, make sure your current bunny is eating well and is healthy.
Before introducing rabbits, get both of them fixed, this can reduce the chance of aggressive behaviors. First, start by side by side enclosures, to get them familiar with each other, introducing the rabbits with their own separate enclosures can allow them to get used to each other while feeling and being safe. There might be some unrest at first, once your rabbits seem comfortable transfer some of the other rabbit’s stuff into the other enclosure to get used to the other rabbit’s scent.
Have a neutral area separate from their enclosures; A separate place that the rabbits aren’t housed, have toys and hiding places so the rabbits have enrichment and can hide if they feel safe. When this is going on, supervise the rabbits, don’t allow negative behavior to escalate to the point of aggression so the rabbits won’t get injured. If after a long period and your rabbits are still not getting along, they might need more time or they could not be compatible. If bonding is going well, gradually increase the time the rabbits are together during daily supervised sessions.
We want positive behavior, such as, sitting or lying next to each other, even when a barrier is between them, grooming each other, seeking each other out for positive interactions, behaving normally around each other. Not negative behavior, such as, chasing each other, mounting, fighting, and growling.
How to Tell Rabbits Have Bonded: once rabbits are spending one to two hours together daily without any problems, they can be introduced into their living space though supervise at the beginning. They can be left alone safely once they’re showing positive behaviors towards one another in that space. If at any time you are confused about your rabbit’s behavior talk to your vet.
